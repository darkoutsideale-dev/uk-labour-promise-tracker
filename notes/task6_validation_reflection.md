# Task 6: Validation, Dashboard, and Reflection

## 1. Status Coding Scheme
| Status | Meaning | Evidence required |
|---|---|---|
| Delivered legally | The promise has clear parliamentary or legal implementation. | Act of Parliament enacted or legal reform completed. |
| Partially delivered / in progress | Some legal or policy action exists, but the full promise is not complete. | Act enacted for part of the promise, or a related bill is still in progress. |
| In progress | Government action has started, but delivery is not complete. | Policy document, bill, funding report, or official statistics. |
| Policy/funding evidence found | The promise can be traced through policy or budget documents, but not through legislation. | Government policy document, funding report, or programme document. |
| No clear evidence yet | No reliable follow-up evidence was found in the current dataset. | No linked Act, bill, policy document, funding source, or outcome indicator. |
| Too early to judge outcomes | Evidence exists, but measurable effects are not yet conclusive. | Statistics exist, but not enough time has passed to assess final delivery. |

## 2. Validation notes
To validate the tracker, we checked whether each promise status was supported by at least one clear source of evidence. The evidence was divided into parliamentary/legal evidence, policy and funding evidence, and emasurable outcome indicators.
This distinction is important becasue an Act of Parliament can show that a promise has been legally delivered,while it does not neessarily prove that the intended housing outcome has already been achieved. For example, the Renters' Rights Act 2025 provides strong legal evidence for pormises H11 and H12, but the wider effects on renters will still need to be measured over time. Similarly, planning-related promises such as H02, H03, H05 and H06 have a strong legal evidence through the PLanning and Infrastructure Act 2025, but actual housing supply outcomes will take longer to appear.
For promises without direct parliamentary evidence, we checked whether they could still be tracked through policy documents, funding reports, or statistics. For example, H01 and H07 are linked to housing supply statistics, ONS housebuilding starts and completions, and government planning or affordable housing documents. this means they are trackable, but they should not yet be judged as fully delivered.

## 3. Dashboard Description
The final tracker links each Labour housing promise to three types of evidence: parliamentary evidence, policy or funding evidence, and measurable outcome evidence. This allows us to see whether a promise has been legally acted on, and also whether it can be followed through a real world indicaotors such as housing supply, affordable housingdelivery, or renters rights implementations.

## 5. Limitations and Reflection
A key limitation of this tracker is that Labour's 2024 government is still relatively new, thus many housing promises cannot be judged through final outcomes. Some of the promises already have a strong legal evidence, for example, the Acts of Parliamnet. Meanwhhile, other are traceable through policy documents, funding reports, or official statistics. However, outcomes such as new homes built, affordable housing delivery, rent affordability , homeless rates or housing supply changes will take longer to measure them. Another limitation is that manifesto promises are often broad. A single promise can involve several froms of evidence such as legislation, budget allocation, local implementation and measurable outcomes. Due to this reason, the tracker should be undersood as monitoring tool rather than a final judgement of success of fail. It is the strongest at showing whether the governmentaction has started and weaker at proving whether long-term housing outcomes have already been achieved.
